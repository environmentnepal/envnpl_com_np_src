# Environmental Justice in Nepal: Origins, Struggles, and Prospects

**Edited by Jonathan K London, Jagannath Adhikari, Thomas Robertson** | 2024 | 298 pages | Routledge | Open Access (CC BY-NC-ND)

## Our Review

For decades, environmental discourse in Nepal has been dominated by technical terms — biodiversity indices, carbon inventories, glacial mass balance measurements. Environmental Justice in Nepal, a landmark open-access volume from Routledge, shifts the lens to something more fundamental: who benefits, who suffers, and who decides.

Edited by Jonathan K London (UC Davis), Jagannath Adhikari (Curtin University), and Thomas Robertson (Worcester Polytechnic), this collection brings together predominantly Nepalese scholars and practitioners to examine environmental questions not as abstract resource management problems but as issues of power, access, and fairness. The timing is significant — Nepal's 2015 constitution enshrined the right to a clean environment, yet the gap between legal promise and lived reality remains vast.

The book is organized into four thematic sections that mirror the country's environmental fault lines: the origins of environmental justice thinking in Nepal, land and agriculture, conservation conflicts, and urban/industrial challenges. Each chapter is grounded in case studies — from the gendered politics of land ownership in the hills to the pesticide burden borne by farmers in the Terai, from water-grabbing in Kathmandu to the dispossession of communities living near protected areas.

### Standout Chapters

**Toward a New Paradigm for Environmental Justice Studies in Nepal** (London & Bhandari) sets the conceptual foundation, arguing that Nepal needs its own environmental justice framework rather than importing Western models. The authors map four dimensions — distributional, procedural, recognition, and capabilities — onto Nepal's unique caste, class, and geographic inequalities.

**From Red to Green to Grey Hills** (Sunita Chaudhary) provides a candid four-decade reflection on community forestry, often celebrated as Nepal's greatest environmental success story. Chaudhary doesn't shy away from uncomfortable questions: if community forestry has been so successful, why do many forest-dependent communities remain among Nepal's poorest?

**Protected Areas and Expendable Communities** (Dhirendra Nalbo) examines human-wildlife conflict compensation near Koshi Tappu Wildlife Reserve. The findings are sobering — compensation mechanisms that look adequate on paper often fail to reach survivors in practice, leaving already-marginalized families to bear the cost of conservation.

Several chapters tackle emerging urban environmental crises — air pollution in Kathmandu, water inequality in rapidly growing cities, and the disproportionate health impacts on informal settlement dwellers. These chapters feel especially urgent as Nepal's urban population has tripled in two decades.

### Why It Matters for Nepal

What makes this volume distinctive is its refusal to separate "environmental" issues from social ones. The chapter connecting Dalit land rights to climate justice makes explicit what is often implicit: the communities most vulnerable to environmental degradation are those already marginalized by caste, class, and gender hierarchies.

The book is also refreshingly honest about failures. Multiple chapters document how well-intentioned policies — community forestry, protected area management, pesticide regulation — have reproduced or even deepened inequalities. This critical stance makes the occasional success stories (joint land ownership as a promising practice for women, community-led water management in some hill districts) feel earned rather than performative.

### Limitations

The volume's geographic coverage is stronger on the central and eastern regions than the far-western and Karnali provinces. Chapters on Indigenous environmental justice, while excellent, could be deepened with more direct engagement with Tharu and Madhesi movements. The urban section, though timely, is thinner than the land and conservation sections — a gap future editions could address.

### Who Should Read It

This is not a book for casual nature enthusiasts. It is, however, essential reading for anyone working at the intersection of development, conservation, and social justice in Nepal — policy makers, NGO workers, researchers, and students of South Asian environmental politics. The open-access format (free to download) makes it especially valuable for Nepalese students and activists who might otherwise be priced out of academic publishing.

## Why We Recommend It

Environmental Justice in Nepal fills a significant gap in the English-language literature on Himalayan environmental politics. It is rigorous without being inaccessible, critical without being cynical, and Nepalese-led without being insular. At a moment when climate adaptation funding is flowing into Nepal at unprecedented levels, the book's core question — who benefits? — has never been more urgent.
