Scientific & traditional knowledge are key for effective & community-centered conservation (commentary)

Read full article at Mongabay Nepal →

A group of women often climbs steep cliffs to set up camera traps for snow leopards around Kibber in Spiti Valley, India. The senior women know the landscape intimately, like what areas the animals use and where cameras are likely to capture movement. They have walked these mountains all their lives and know how to navigate the most rugged and inaccessible terrain safely, even in snow and ice. This is a kind of access that visiting researchers simply cannot replicate.

Back at the village, they gather around two computer screens. The younger women, who are typically more adept at handling technology, organize and sort the photographic data. Together, they identify individual snow leopards from their coat patterns, building scientific monitoring skills over time. Their work contributes directly to regional and global assessment and monitoring of snow leopard populations.

Who are these scientists conducting camera-trap monitoring of snow leopards? They are not trained researchers. They are women from the Indigenous community of Kibber in Spiti Valley. This work is done alongside their other chores: Livestock rearing, farming, household responsibilities, and community work. They do this, because they are interested in knowing how many snow leopards they are helping protect on their community lands; they take pride in learning research techniques; and they see themselves as part of the global effort to study and protect them.

Lobzang Yangchen, the coordinator of the Kibber team of 11 women, says, “I started as a volunteer because I was curious about snow leopards. Now it has become my livelihood. There isn’t much else to do during winters, so this is when we do the camera trapping work.”

These women work alongside trained researchers and conservation practitioners in a genuine partnership. Their community has spent decades building conservation initiatives. When research showed that wild herbivore populations were kept low by limited availability of forage, they set aside more than 35 square kilometers (13.5 square miles) of pasture — over 20% of their total land — as a livestock-free reserve. As a result,populations of blue sheep, the snow leopard’s primary wild prey, are now five times greater than before.
