The Snow Leopard

The Snow Leopard

Peter Matthiessen | 1978 | 338 pages

Our Review

In 1973, Peter Matthiessen joined field biologist George Schaller on an expedition into the Dolpo region of Nepal, deep in the Himalayas, to study the Himalayan blue sheep — and perhaps glimpse the snow leopard, one of the world's most elusive creatures.

What follows is not just a travelogue or a nature journal. It's a deeply personal meditation on loss (Matthiessen had recently lost his wife), on Zen Buddhism, and on what it means to be human in relationship to the natural world.

The snow leopard remains tantalizingly just out of sight throughout the book — much like enlightenment itself in Buddhist tradition. Matthiessen's prose is luminous: precise about the landscape, honest about his own grief, and reverent toward the mountains that surround him.

"The sun is round. I ring with life, and the mountains ring, and when I can hear it, there is a ringing that we share. I understand all this, not in my mind but in my heart, know how meaningless it is to try to capture what cannot be expressed, know that mere words will remain when I read it all again, another day."

Why We Recommend It

The Snow Leopard won the National Book Award in 1979 and has never been out of print. For anyone interested in Nepal's environment, it's essential reading — a window into the high Himalayas before climate change accelerated glacial melt, and a reminder that conservation is inseparable from human culture and spirit.

Themes: Nepal, Himalayas, wildlife conservation, Buddhism, grief and healing, mountaineering
