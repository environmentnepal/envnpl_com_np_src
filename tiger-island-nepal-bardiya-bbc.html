Tiger Island: Inside Nepal's Record-Breaking Tiger Sanctuary

On a small, tangled island in the river systems of western Nepal, something extraordinary is happening. Bardiya National Park is now home to one of the highest concentrations of tigers anywhere on Earth — and for the first time, a BBC film crew has captured their hidden lives using an unprecedented fleet of drones.

Goma, the matriarch tigress of Tiger Island. BBC/Tiger Island

The Discovery

In 2023, wildlife filmmaker Patrick Evans was in Nepal filming rhinos for the BBC series Asia when he received a tip from his executive producer: Bardiya National Park had seen a sixfold increase in tiger numbers. The BBC News Nepal team had been there and documented a remarkable recovery. But filming tigers is notoriously difficult — one crew spent over 700 days following tigers without once capturing them hunting.

Evans flew across Nepal to see for himself. Within two days, he saw three tigers. "I count this as my first real sighting," he wrote, "because I was able to see its blazing eyes. I found myself truly shocked at its enormous size — it felt comparable to watching a horse crossing the landscape."

That encounter launched a two-year project that became Tiger Island, a groundbreaking BBC documentary that used an entire fleet of drones to follow individual tigers through two separate months of filming — a world first for wildlife filmmaking.

Drone footage and remote cameras revealed unprecedented tiger behaviour. BBC/Tiger Island

Meet the Tigers

The crew identified individual tigers by their stripe patterns — nature's barcodes, unique to each animal. Here are the residents of Tiger Island:

Goma — The nine-year-old matriarch. Estimated to be the largest and most experienced tigress on the island, Goma has successfully raised multiple litters to adulthood. Local wildlife guides Sushila and Manju Mahatara named her after their grandmother and have observed her for years. At the time of filming, she had two nine-month-old cubs.

Mala — Goma's four-year-old daughter. Typically, young tigers leave their mothers at around two years of age. Mala inherited part of her mother's territory — a common behaviour among solitary big cats where mothers cede territory to daughters to minimise conflict. As a first-time mother with three cubs aged just three months, Mala faced a steep learning curve.

Jugini — Possibly another daughter of Goma's, and her neighbour to the north. She interacts peacefully with the other tigresses, especially Goma. At the time of filming, Jugini had three cubs of her own.

Bandheil — The only known male tiger controlling territory on the island. Adult male tigers in Nepal typically exceed 200 kilograms, and Bandheil is one of the largest seen in the area. Male tigers defend territories up to 18 miles across and may not play a role in cub-rearing — and may even kill cubs they encounter.

Bandheil, the dominant male, controls territory across the entire island. BBC/Tiger Island

Technology Meets Conservation

What makes Tiger Island unique is the technology behind it. The crew deployed an entire fleet of drones — a world-first approach for tiger filming — allowing them to follow individual animals through dense vegetation that would be impossible to navigate on foot. Remote camera traps supplemented the aerial footage, building a detailed picture of the tigers' movements, relationships, and hunting strategies.

The drone footage revealed behaviours never documented before: tender moments between Goma and her cubs, tense encounters between rival tigresses, and the extraordinary distances Bandheil covers in a single night — up to 18 miles.

Nepal's Conservation Success

Bardiya's tiger recovery is one of the world's great conservation stories. Nepal nearly doubled its tiger population between 2010 and 2022, from 121 to 355 individuals, putting it on track to meet the global TX2 goal of doubling wild tiger numbers. Bardiya alone saw its tiger population increase sixfold.

The success is built on community-based conservation. Local guides like the Mahatara sisters — Sushila, Manju, and Ranju — are central to both the research and the filming. Their intimate knowledge of individual tigers, passed down through years of patient observation, made the BBC's work possible.

The sisters carry stout wooden sticks as their only defense while walking barefoot through tiger territory — a powerful reminder that Nepal's conservation success depends on people living alongside predators at close quarters.

What's Next

With Goma confirmed to have mated with Bandheil, a new generation of cubs will soon arrive on Tiger Island. The BBC team plans to return, documenting how the next chapter unfolds for Nepal's most famous tiger family.

Tiger Island and the Asia episode "Tangled Worlds" are available on BBC iPlayer. For Nepal, it's a story of what's possible when conservation, technology, and local knowledge come together — and proof that even the most elusive animals can reveal their secrets when we find new ways of looking.
